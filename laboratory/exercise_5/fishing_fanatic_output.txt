## Top Baits for Pike

### 1. Live Bait (Often the Most Effective)

Pike are opportunistic predators, and nothing beats the natural look, smell, and movement of a struggling baitfish.
*   **Best Choices:**
    *   **Shiners/Golden Shiners:** A classic for a reason. They're active and attractive.
    *   **Suckers:** Especially larger ones, excellent for targeting bigger pike.
    *   **Perch/Sunfish:** Where legal, these are natural forage and highly effective.
    *   **Chubs:** Another common and robust baitfish.
    *   **Smelt:** Particularly good in areas where smelt are part of the pike's diet.
*   **How to Fish:** Often suspended under a bobber/float or on a bottom rig with a quick-strike leader to prevent deep hooking.
*   **Considerations:** Check local regulations regarding the use and transport of live bait, as some species or origins may be prohibited.

### 2. Dead Bait (Especially for Larger Pike or Cold Water)

Often overlooked, dead bait can be incredibly effective, especially for wary, larger pike or in colder water when they're less inclined to chase. The strong scent trail is a major attractor.
*   **Best Choices:**
    *   **Mackerel/Herring:** Oily fish that release a strong scent. Often available frozen at tackle shops or grocery stores.
    *   **Smelt:** Fresh or frozen, their scent is appealing.
    *   **Local Forage (Dead):** A dead shiner, sucker, or perch is often irresistible.
*   **How to Fish:** On a quick-strike rig, either ledgered on the bottom or suspended under a large float. Allowing it to lay on the bottom for a while can attract pike from a distance.
*   **Considerations:** Use a quick-strike rig to minimize deep hooking, as pike tend to engulf dead bait more quickly.

### 3. Artificial Lures (Most Popular for Active Angling)

Artificials allow for covering water, targeting specific depths, and eliciting reaction strikes.

*   **Spoons:**
    *   **Why:** Flash, wobble, casts a mile. Mimics an injured baitfish.

*   **Spinnerbaits/Inline Spinners:**
    *   **Why:** Vibration, flash, great for fishing in and around weeds.
    *   **Popular Choices:** Mepps Musky Killer, Blue Fox Vibrax, Booyah Pikee.
    *   **Colors:** Silver, gold, chartreuse, white, black.
*   **Crankbaits/Jerkbaits:**
    *   **Why:** Mimic baitfish with various swimming actions and depths. Jerkbaits are great for erratic, wounded baitfish presentations.
    *   **Popular Choices:** Rapala (Shad Rap, X-Rap, Husky Jerk), Lucky Craft Pointer, Storm ThunderStick.

    *   **Warm Water (Summer):** Faster retrieves with spoons, spinners, crankbaits, and active live bait.
*   **Water Clarity:**
    *   **Clear Water:** Natural colors, realistic patterns.
    *   **Murky Water:** Brighter colors (chartreuse, firetiger), high-contrast patterns, or baits with strong vibration/scent.
*   **Target Size:** Generally, bigger baits attract bigger pike.
*   **Regulations:** Always check local fishing laws regarding bait use, especially live bait.

Salmon fishing is an incredibly diverse and rewarding pursuit, captivating anglers worldwide with the powerful fight of these iconic fish and the beauty of the environments they inhabit. From the vastness of the ocean to the intimate flow of a freshwater stream, catching salmon offers a unique blend of challenge, skill, and connection with nature.

Let's break down what salmon fishing entails:

## 1. Salmon Species

There are several species of salmon, each with its own characteristics, preferred habitats, and fishing techniques:

**Pacific Salmon (Genus *Oncorhynchus*)**
*   **Chinook Salmon (King Salmon):** The largest and often most prized Pacific salmon, known for their immense power and stubborn fight. They can reach over 50 pounds.
*   **Coho Salmon (Silver Salmon):** Known for their acrobatic jumps and aggressive strikes. They are excellent sport fish and highly sought after.
*   **Sockeye Salmon (Red Salmon):** Famous for their vibrant red flesh and strong runs. They are primarily plankton feeders, making them harder to catch on traditional lures, often targeted by "flossing" or drift fishing in rivers.
*   **Pink Salmon (Humpy Salmon):** The smallest and most abundant Pacific salmon, characterized by a prominent hump on their backs during spawning. They provide fast action and are great for beginners.
*   **Chum Salmon (Dog Salmon):** Known for their large teeth and distinct markings during spawning. They are powerful fighters and are increasingly popular with sport anglers.

**Atlantic Salmon (Genus *Salmo salar*)**
*   Unlike Pacific salmon, Atlantic salmon can often survive spawning and return to the ocean to spawn multiple times. They are highly prized for their elegance, powerful runs, and often targeted with fly fishing in pristine rivers across Eastern Canada, Europe, and the US (Maine).

**Landlocked Salmon**
*   Some populations of Atlantic Salmon become landlocked in freshwater lakes (e.g., in New England, Great Lakes), adapting to a purely freshwater existence.

## 2. Where to Find Them

Salmon are anadromous, meaning they spend most of their lives in saltwater (oceans, large lakes) and return to freshwater (rivers, streams) to spawn.

*   **Saltwater (Ocean/Coastal):** Anglers target salmon in bays, estuaries, and open ocean environments as they feed and mature. This is where they grow largest.
*   **Freshwater (Rivers/Streams):** As salmon migrate upstream to their spawning grounds, they enter rivers, offering incredible opportunities for shore-based or boat-based fishing.
*   **Great Lakes:** Stocked populations of Chinook, Coho, and Atlantic salmon thrive in the Great Lakes, providing a robust fishery similar to ocean fishing in terms of methods and tackle.

## 3. Common Fishing Methods

The technique used often depends on the species, location, and time of year.

*   **Trolling (Saltwater & Great Lakes):** The most common method in large bodies of water. Anglers use boats to drag lures, bait (like herring), or flashers/dodgers at various depths using downriggers, divers, or lead core lines. This covers a lot of water to find active fish.
*   **Fly Fishing (Rivers):** A classic and highly refined method, especially for Atlantic salmon, Sockeye, and river-run Coho/Chinook. Techniques include:
    *   **Single-hand casting:** Using traditional fly rods.
    *   **Spey Casting:** Using two-handed rods to cast long lines in tight spaces, ideal for large rivers.
    *   Flies can imitate insects (though less common for feeding salmon), baitfish, or simply provoke a strike (intruder patterns, large streamers).
*   **Spin Fishing / Gear Fishing (Rivers & Lakes):** Very versatile and popular.
    *   **Casting & Retrieving:** Spoons, spinners, and plugs (like Kwikfish or Wiggle Warts) are cast out and reeled in to imitate baitfish or attract an aggressive strike.
    *   **Drift Fishing / "Boondogging":** Using natural bait (salmon roe, cured shrimp) or artificial presentations (drift bobbers, beads) that drift naturally with the current, often with minimal weight.
    *   **Jigging:** Using metal jigs or lead-head jigs with soft plastics from boats or shore, especially effective in deeper pools or estuaries.
    *   **Bottom Bouncing:** Using heavy weights to keep bait or lures near the river bottom in strong currents.
*   **Plunking (Rivers):** A stationary method where heavy weights hold bait or lures in the current while anglers wait for a fish to hit.

## 4. Essential Gear

*   **Rods & Reels:** Varies greatly by method and species.
    *   **Trolling:** Heavy-duty conventional or baitcasting rods and reels designed for powerful fish and heavy lines.
    *   **Fly Fishing:** 7 to 10-weight fly rods (single or two-handed) with large arbor reels holding plenty of backing.
    *   **Spin Fishing:** Medium-heavy to heavy spinning or baitcasting rods, often 8-10 feet for river fishing to aid in line control and casting distance.
*   **Line:** Braided line (for sensitivity and strength), monofilament (for shock absorption), or fluorocarbon (for invisibility) are all used, often in combination.
*   **Lures & Baits:** Spoons (e.g.,ダーウィンのスプーン), spinners (e.g., Mepps, Rooster Tail), plugs (e.g., Kwikfish, Hot Shot), jigs, soft plastics, salmon roe, herring, anchovies, worms.
*   **Terminal Tackle:** Swivels, leaders, hooks (single, treble, barbless), weights (pencil lead, slinkies, cannonball).
*   **Nets:** Large, sturdy landing nets are crucial for safely landing and handling powerful salmon.
*   **Waders & Boots:** Essential for river anglers accessing prime wading spots.
*   **Boat & Electronics:** For trolling, a boat with a good motor, fish finder, and GPS is invaluable.

## 5. Timing is Key (The "Runs")

Salmon fishing is highly seasonal, driven by the fish's migratory "runs" back to their natal rivers. These runs occur at different times of the year depending on the species and location. Anglers closely monitor fishing reports, river conditions (water levels, temperature), and tide charts to time their outings.

## 6. Regulations and Conservation

*   **Licenses:** A valid fishing license is almost always required.
*   **Limits:** Daily catch limits and possession limits are strictly enforced.
*   **Size Restrictions:** Minimum and/or maximum size limits may apply.
*   **Gear Restrictions:** Regulations might specify hook types (e.g., barbless), bait restrictions (e.g., no live bait), or specific fishing hours.
*   **Catch & Release:** Practicing responsible catch and release, especially for wild or endangered stocks, is crucial for conservation. Proper handling techniques minimize harm to the fish.

Salmon populations face challenges from habitat loss, climate change, and commercial fishing pressure. Sport anglers often play a vital role in conservation efforts through license fees and advocacy.

## The Thrill of the Fight

What makes salmon fishing so addictive?

*   **The Power:** Salmon are incredibly strong, offering acrobatic jumps, long runs, and stubborn head shakes.
*   **The Chase:** The anticipation of a bite, the sudden jolt, and the ensuing battle are exhilarating.
*   **The Environment:** Salmon fishing often takes you to breathtakingly beautiful locations, from wild coastlines to serene mountain rivers.
*   **The Reward:** Beyond the fight, salmon are highly prized table fare, and landing a chrome-bright fish is a deeply satisfying experience.

Whether you're a seasoned angler chasing a trophy Chinook in the Pacific or a beginner hoping to catch your first Pink salmon in a small river, salmon fishing offers a truly unforgettable adventure.
